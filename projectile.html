Equations of Motion

x = x0 + vxt
y = y0 + vyt − ½gt2

Horizontal motion is constant velocity. Vertical motion is constant acceleration due to gravity (g = 9.8 m/s²).

Components

vx = v cos(θ)
vy = v sin(θ)

The initial velocity vector is split into horizontal and vertical components using the launch angle.

Key Quantities

Range = (v² sin(2θ)) / g (level ground)
Max height = y0 + (vy²) / (2g)
Time of flight depends on launch height and vertical velocity.

Tips

Drag the cannon anywhere on the canvas. Change the angle to see how 45° gives the longest range on level ground. Add height to clear obstacles.

Check Your Understanding

Test your knowledge of projectile motion. Click an answer to see feedback.

1. On level ground, a projectile launched at 60° has the same range as one launched at which angle?
30° — Correct! Complementary angles (summing to 90°) produce the same range on level ground. 60° + 30° = 90°, so they have equal range.
Not quite. 15° and 60° do not sum to 90°. Try thinking about complementary angles that add up to 90°.
Close! 45° gives the maximum range, but it's not the complement of 60°. 60° + 30° = 90°.
Not quite. 75° and 60° sum to 135°, not 90°. Complementary angles (sum = 90°) have the same range.
2. If you double the launch velocity (keeping the same angle and height), the range will:
Quadruple — Correct! Range ∝ v² (R = v² sin(2θ) / g). Doubling v makes R four times larger.
Almost! Range is proportional to v², not v. Try again: what is 2²?
Not quite. Velocity is a key factor in the range equation. Think about what happens when you square a doubled number.
Not quite. Range scales with the square of velocity (v²). Doubling v means the range gets multiplied by 2² = 4.
3. What happens to the time of flight if you increase the launch height (keeping angle and speed the same)?
It increases — Correct! A higher launch point means the projectile has farther to fall to reach the ground, so it stays in the air longer.
Actually, it's the opposite. Think about it: if you throw a ball from a higher platform, does it land sooner or later?
Not quite. The vertical motion equation is y = y₀ + vyt - ½gt². A larger y₀ means it takes more time for y to reach 0.
The effect is consistent regardless of angle — more height always means more time. But the amount of increase does depend on vertical velocity!
4. At what launch angle are the horizontal and vertical components of velocity equal (ignoring the sign)?
45° — Correct! At 45°, cos(45°) = sin(45°) = √2/2, so vx = v cos(θ) = v sin(θ) = vy.
Not quite. At 30°, cos(30°) ≈ 0.866 and sin(30°) = 0.5. These aren't equal. Try the angle where sin and cos are the same.
Not quite. At 60°, cos(60°) = 0.5 and sin(60°) ≈ 0.866. These aren't equal. Try the angle where sin and cos are the same.
At 90°, cos(90°) = 0 and sin(90°) = 1. There's no horizontal velocity at all! Try the angle where sin and cos are equal.
